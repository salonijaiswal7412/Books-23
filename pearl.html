The pearl that broke its shell

AUTHOR:NADIA HASHIMI

"The Pearl That Broke Its Shell" is a novel written by Nadia Hashimi. The book was first published in 2014. Set in Afghanistan, it weaves together the stories of two Afghan women, one from the early 20th century and the other from the present day. The novel's title is a metaphor for the practice of bacha posh, a cultural custom where families without sons dress one of their daughters as a boy, allowing her certain freedoms and privileges traditionally reserved for males. The "pearl" in this context represents the disguised daughter, and the "shell" refers to the traditional gender roles and societal expectations that the practice challenges. The two main characters are Rahima and Shekiba. Rahima's story is set in contemporary Afghanistan, while Shekiba's narrative takes place a century earlier. Rahima's family, unable to produce sons and facing societal pressures, decides to turn her into a bacha posh, enabling her to attend school and work. Shekiba, on the other hand, adopts a similar role due to tragic circumstances, and her story provides historical context to the cultural practice. "The Pearl That Broke Its Shell" offers readers a glimpse into the challenges faced by Afghan women across different time periods and sheds light on the complexities of gender roles and societal expectations in the region.
