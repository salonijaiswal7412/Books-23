The picture of Dorian Gray

AUTHOR:OSCAR WILDE

"The Picture of Dorian Gray" is a novel written by Oscar Wilde, first published in 1890. It is considered one of Wilde's most significant works and a classic of Victorian literature. The novel is known for its exploration of aestheticism, morality, and the consequences of a hedonistic lifestyle. The story revolves around the character of Dorian Gray, a young and handsome man living in Victorian-era London. Dorian sits for a portrait painted by the talented artist Basil Hallward. During the painting session, Dorian expresses a desire to remain forever young while his portrait ages instead. His wish is mysteriously granted, and the portrait becomes a reflection of his moral and physical degradation. As Dorian pursues a life of pleasure and indulgence, he remains outwardly unchanged, while the portrait becomes a grotesque and corrupted representation of his true self. The novel delves into themes of vanity, the pursuit of beauty, and the consequences of immoral behavior. Wilde uses "The Picture of Dorian Gray" to critique the superficiality of society and the dangers of unchecked hedonism. The novel is also known for its witty and epigrammatic style, which is characteristic of Wilde's writing.
